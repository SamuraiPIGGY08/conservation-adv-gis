GIS in Conservation

From map to movement

Six decades of geographic technology transforming how we protect the planet

1964
The world's first GIS
Roger Tomlinson builds the Canada Geographic Information System — the first operational GIS. Designed for land-use planning, it introduces layering spatial data to reveal patterns no single map could show.

1972
NASA Landsat launches
The first Landsat satellite begins continuous Earth observation. Scientists can now monitor deforestation and land change from space — opening long-term environmental monitoring to the world.

1982
Esri releases ArcInfo
Esri's ArcInfo becomes the first commercially successful GIS software. Conservation organizations adopt GIS for habitat mapping and protected area planning beyond what government agencies could do alone.

1994
Habitat suitability modeling
ArcGIS enables habitat suitability modeling — combining land cover, elevation, and proximity data to predict where species can survive. Conservation shifts from mapping boundaries to modeling future landscapes.

2003
USGS NLCD and gap analysis
USGS publishes the National Land Cover Database. Gap analysis programs use these layers to identify ecosystems underrepresented in protected area networks and direct where conservation funding should go.

IUCN launches the WDPA
IUCN and Protected Planet launch the World Database on Protected Areas — the first global database of protected land and sea. Conservation planners can now identify gaps in biodiversity coverage worldwide.

Landsat archive opens free
NASA makes the entire Landsat archive freely available. Decades of Earth observation data — previously locked behind cost — become accessible to any researcher. Long-term land change analysis scales globally.

Rise of citizen science platforms
iNaturalist, GBIF, and eBird turn the public into data contributors. Research-grade, georeferenced species observations accumulate at scales no field survey could match — giving conservation GIS a live global data layer.
