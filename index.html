The evolution of GIS in conservation

From a Canadian land-use project to real-time global monitoring

1964 The first GIS — Canada Geographic Information System
Roger Tomlinson
Father of GIS

Roger Tomlinson built the Canada Geographic Information System (CGIS) — the first operational GIS ever created. Its original purpose was land-use planning for the Canadian government.

Conservation organizations used early GIS primarily for land classification and basic environmental data collection. Only large government agencies could afford access — but the concept of layering multiple spatial datasets to find landscape patterns was born here.

two decades of computing growth
1980s – 90s ArcGIS + Landsat — conservation goes digital
NASA Landsat
1972 – present

Esri released ArcInfo and later ArcGIS, making spatial modeling accessible beyond governments. NASA's Landsat program opened its archive, enabling long-term environmental monitoring at regional and global scales.

Conservation organizations began analyzing habitat loss, forest depletion, and land fragmentation over time — not just mapping where things were, but tracking how they were changing year to year.

internet era + open data movement
2000s Landscape scale — ecosystems and gap analysis
USGS NLCD
National land cover

GIS expanded from parcel-level mapping to full landscape and ecosystem management. Gap analysis programs identified ecosystems underrepresented in protected area networks.

USGS published national land cover maps and elevation data. The World Database on Protected Areas (WDPA), maintained by IUCN and Protected Planet, enabled global-scale biodiversity planning for the first time.

smartphones + cloud computing
2010s Citizen science + open data — the iNaturalist era
iNaturalist
Citizen science platform

Platforms like iNaturalist transformed biodiversity data collection. Anyone with a smartphone could contribute georeferenced species observations — generating spatially explicit datasets at scales no field survey could match alone.

When validated for research quality, citizen science data became a legitimate source for spatial analysis. Conservation GIS gained a new, continuously updated input layer tied directly to public engagement.

drones · LiDAR · machine learning
Today Real-time monitoring — drones, LiDAR, machine learning
Drones + LiDAR
Precision habitat mapping

Drone imagery, LiDAR point clouds, and machine learning classification now enable precision habitat mapping at centimeter resolution. Cloud platforms allow real-time data sharing between agencies worldwide.

Wildfire modeling, climate vulnerability assessments, biodiversity tracking, and ecosystem resilience planning all run on the same foundational concept Tomlinson introduced in 1964 — where things are, and how they're changing.
